Verbs and verb phrases

So, what can we say about English verbs? Here's a list of nice verby verbs, really typical ones:

  • jump, walk, ask, turn, work, move, play, pull, reach, laugh

Are they action words? They sure are. Did you notice that this could be a list of nouns though?

  • she missed the jump
  • I went for a walk
  • that's a pretty big ask
  • give it a turn and see
  • I went to work
  • etc.

Each of the underlined words above is a noun. If you don't believe me, try thinking about the characteristics we studied earlier. But in the following cases, they're verbs.

  • she jumped< the gap
  • I walk the dog each morning
  • he's asking for a $10,000 raise
  • it's hard to turn the knob
  • I work in Toronto

So, how can we tell the difference between nouns and verbs? Sometimes we can't until they're in context. The list above is perfectly ambiguous. Nevertheless, verbs generally have the following properties (again, example follow):

  • Verbs typically denote actions and states, and they do not denote physical objects.
  • A word that has a past-tense form is a verb (but not all verbs have past-tense forms).
  • Almost every verb has an -s form (but not all).
  • Almost every verb has an -ing form (but not all).
  • Almost every verb can follow the infinitive marker to.
  • A verb cannot be modified by an adjective phrase (never ever... I think).
  • A verb can typically be modified by an adverb phrase.
  • A verb phrase must be headed by a verb (always).
  • A clause must be headed by a verb phrase (always).
  • A verb is often in an agreement relationship with a phrase functioning as the subject of the clause.
