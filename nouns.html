Nouns and noun phrases


Gigas Devil, CC BY-SA 4.0, via Wikimedia Commons

There is no perfect definition of noun, just as there is no perfect definition of soup, but we can get very close, as long as we are willing to hedge with words like can, almost, and typically. Examples follow below.

I will use words here that have not yet been defined. This may be frustrating, but it is inevitable. Syntax is a system, and its elements can only be defined in relation to each other. The meaning of the other words will become clear soon.

  • Nouns can denote almost anything from objects to actions to characteristics.
  • A word that denotes a person, place, or thing is a noun (but not "a noun is a word that denotes person, place, or thing").
  • A word that is countable is a noun (but not "all nouns are countable").
  • Almost any word that is plural is a noun (but not "all nouns can be plural").
  • Nouns can typically follow the or a (but not "words following the or a are nouns").
  • Nouns can typically be modified by adjective phrases (but not "words modified by adjective phrases are nouns").
  • Nouns cannot typically be modified by adverb phrases.
  • Nouns head noun phrases.
  • Noun phrases can perform a variety of functions, including subject and object (but not “subjects and objects are noun phrases”).
  • Noun phrases can typically be possessive (but not “possessives are noun phrases”).

Importantly, each of these characteristics can be unclear to English-language learners. It's also important to note that many of the statements are not commutative (multiplication is commutative but division isn't; X × Y = Y × X but X ÷ Y ≠ Y ÷ X).
